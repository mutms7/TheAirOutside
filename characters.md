# Characters

Six characters. Each entry covers: **surface persona**, **hidden interior**, **specific small moments that reveal interior indirectly** (show, don't tell), **how their suit reflects pretend-self vs. actual-self**, and **their arc**.

Names are working drafts — revisit before Phase 2.

---

## 1. Wren Hata — Protagonist

**Age**: 15. First year of high school (this culture starts secondary school at 14; suits become socially mandatory around 12).

**Surface**: A "good student." Quiet voice (run through a voice-modulator set slightly warmer than their real pitch). Follows trends accurately. Never causes friction. Polite to everyone. Liked by Tae, respected by teachers, unbothered by Cael because Wren's decorations are correctly on-trend and they don't draw negative attention.

**Hidden interior**: A slow-growing flatness they can't name. A persistent dull inattention to their own feelings — not depression, just *absence*. They have never imagined being without their suit. They have also never imagined being themselves, separately from it. The most honest thing about Wren at the start is that they are an *earnest* conformist — they are not cynical, they have not seen through the system; they have just never seen the system at all.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- Pretends to be: someone whose ease in this world is genuine, who chose this peach-and-mint palette, who finds the sticker pleasing.
- Actually is: someone who has never been asked who they are, and who would not know how to begin answering. The suit is, more than anything, *the conversation Wren has never had with themselves*.

**Small revealing moments (show, don't tell)**:
- In the cold open, before answering Tae's morning ping, Wren looks at the ant hill for two seconds longer than necessary. The game tracks nothing. The player just sees them look.
- Wren laughs at a Cael-style joke they don't find funny. The suit's bio-monitor registers a tiny vital irregularity. No one comments on it. The HUD doesn't even highlight it. It is just there for the careful player.
- Wren never finishes a sentence about themselves. The suit's auto-pause-comma jumps in — a feature that lets you "pause to compose your thought." Wren uses it constantly. Other characters do not.
- At the end of day one, alone in their bedroom, Wren sits at their desk for a full minute doing nothing. The screen does not narrate this. No thought-bubble. Just sitting.

**Arc**: Earnest performance → noticing (the ant hill, the rain, the song, Iris) → reaching (the choice to seek Iris, or to be alone with themselves) → bare presence (the climax) → a new kind of attention they carry forward, regardless of what they wear next.

## 2. Tae Vance — Best friend

**Age**: 15. Same year as Wren. Has known Wren since they were 11.

**Surface**: Confident, fashionable, generous with attention. The one in their friend group who knows what is *in* this week. Maintains Wren's decoration with affection. Genuinely likes Wren — this is important. Tae is not using Wren cynically. The kindness is real.

**Hidden interior**: Terrified of being plain. Her suit is the most-decorated in their friend group, and she replaces decorations more often than anyone, because she is never quite sure what is *enough*. She has internalized that her value is her brightness. Going dull is, to her, a kind of death. She does not know this consciously. She would deny it if asked.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- Pretends to be: someone who delights in style for its own sake.
- Actually is: someone using style as armor against being seen as forgettable.

**Small revealing moments**:
- Tae tells a self-deprecating joke that lands too true. She moves on so fast no one catches it.
- In her drawer (visible during scene 10), there is an old un-applied sticker — last year's trend — that she has never put on and never thrown away. She has never told anyone why she keeps it.
- When Wren shows up one day without their usual decoration, Tae's *first* instinct — visible in her micro-expression — is to replace it *before anyone else can see*. Not to ask why.
- Tae compliments other people's suits constantly. She rarely compliments their faces.

**Arc**: Tae does not step outside. The story does not require her to. Her arc is smaller and quieter: when she sees Wren step outside at the end, she does not report them and she does not look away. That is her grace. She may, off-screen, eventually do something with the drawer. The story does not show this. *Hummel principle* — let the audience carry it.

## 3. Iris Quint — The one who has already left

**Age**: 17. Two years older than Wren. A junior.

**Surface**: A whispered-about figure. The kids call her "the unmasked one," sometimes other things. She is not popular and not unpopular — she is *not part of the system anymore*. She does not seek company. She is not a recruiter. She does not have a manifesto. She is a little awkward in conversation; she lost some of her social vocabulary when she stepped out.

**Hidden interior**: Still figuring it out herself. She left her suit because of a private grief that she is not finished processing. She does not have the answers. What she has is *the experience of having tried*, which is more than most people in this school have. She is lonely sometimes. She is also at peace in a way the other students cannot recognize, and so they read it as sadness.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- When she wears it (she still has to, for some classes, for some social situations), it is unadorned, slightly outgrown, the lack of decoration the loudest thing in any room she enters.
- Pretends to be: nothing. This is most of the point. She is trying very hard to *just be*, which is harder than it sounds.
- Actually is: a 17-year-old carrying her own grief and trying to figure out how to be next to other people while bare. The dignity is real. The vulnerability is also real. The story respects both.

**Small revealing moments**:
- The first time Wren sees Iris outside her suit, Iris is just looking at a windowpane. No agenda. She turns and notices Wren noticing, and does not say anything. The silence is offered, not demanded.
- Iris's bare hands have small marks on them — old scars on the knuckles, a cut that healed wrong. She does not hide them. Nobody else has hands like that visible at school.
- Once, in a hallway, Iris and the janitor hum the same tune at the same time. They do not look at each other. They have an arrangement that has never been spoken.
- When Iris finally tells Wren what made her step out, it is a single sentence. No more. The story does not let her over-explain.

**Arc**: She has already arced. Her function in the story is to be witnessed and to witness. But the player learns, in the climax, what made her step out — and that revelation is also her *receiving* something: someone has finally asked, and she has finally been seen by someone who can hold it.

## 4. Mr. Ozaki — The dexterity instructor

**Age**: Late 40s.

**Surface**: The dexterity-training teacher. Mild, patient, slightly old-fashioned. Wears last season's suit, undecorated, the unmodified factory finish. The kids think it is because he does not care.

**Hidden interior**: He used to be a dancer. Long story. He stayed inside his suit because of a private decision he is not eager to discuss — it involves someone he lost, and a promise he made. He cares more than anyone about what the suits do to a body's memory of itself. He is, in a quiet way, the only adult in the school who knows what the climax of Wren's story is going to be — though he never names it and never instructs it.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- Pretends to be: a generic mid-level teacher in a workmanlike job who hasn't kept up with fashion.
- Actually is: someone who has decided to stay inside, with full awareness, for reasons that are his own. His unadorned suit is not laziness. It is a discipline.

**Small revealing moments**:
- In a dexterity drill, he steps out of his suit briefly to demonstrate a movement and steps back in. The whole class falls silent. He says, "It's faster to show you." The understatement is the joke and also not the joke.
- On a worksheet someone left in his classroom, he has corrected a student's poem about suits with a single pencil note: *be more honest*.
- He hums the song from Motif 4. The player may or may not catch this on first playthrough.
- He gives Wren one specific small gift in scene 8 — a sentence from a passage he reads aloud — and never refers to it again.

**Arc**: He does not change. His function is to be the adult who *sees* before Wren can name what is happening, and to give Wren one thing at the right time. He is the story's quiet permission.

## 5. Cael Renfrew — The popular cruel one

**Age**: 16. Sophomore.

**Surface**: Charismatic. The center of attention. A casual gleeful bully toward kids whose suits aren't up to standard. Loud, expensively customized, the apparent winner of the social game.

**Hidden interior**: Not particularly evil. Very, very afraid of being on the wrong side of the in-group. He performs harder than anyone. His suit's customization is *bought*, not designed — someone else picked it for him too, just like Tae picks for Wren. He has been afraid of being uncool since he was nine. He is also a casualty of the same machine that produced Wren — he is what happens to someone who never finds the door.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- Pretends to be: the one in charge of what is cool.
- Actually is: a kid being told what is cool by people he is too afraid to disappoint.

**Small revealing moments**:
- Cael mocks someone for an undecorated shoulder, then privately, the next day, worries that his *own* shoulder decoration is out of date.
- He laughs at Iris when she passes. He does not look at her face when he does.
- In one optional scene (texture, not gate), the player can witness him alone after school, applying a new sticker carefully, hands almost shaking. He is not performing. The scene is short and the player walks past. He never knows he was seen.

**Arc**: He does not step outside. The story does not redeem him in any meaningful sense — that would be a lie. But the *one moment alone with the sticker* is the story's quiet acknowledgment that he is also a person. The audience carries this. Cael does not.

## 6. The janitor — Never named in dialogue

**Age**: Middle-aged. Probably 50s.

**Surface**: Invisible to most students. Including, initially, to Wren. Wears a service-class suit with minimal decoration. Hums while she works.

**Hidden interior**: A complete person whom the story does not need to fully explain. She has a life outside the school — a household, friends, a routine, the specific reasons she chose this work, the specific reasons she still hums the song. The story respects her by **not** flattening her into a symbol. She is not the protagonist's spirit guide. She is just a person who does her job and is herself, completely unconcerned with being noticed.

**Suit (pretend vs. actual)**:
- She is not pretending. She does not have the budget or the interest to perform. Her suit is functional. This is itself a position the rest of the school has lost the language to read.

**Small revealing moments**:
- The first time Wren passes her, the camera-frame deliberately does not center her. She is at the edge. The player may not even register her presence.
- She hums the song from Motif 4. She is the second person to hum it on-screen.
- She watches Iris pass with calm familiarity. They have an arrangement. They have never spoken about it on screen.
- In the coda, if Wren has stepped outside, she sees Wren and gives a single small acknowledgment — a nod, a held look, no more. One of the most emotionally weighted beats in the script. *This is the 1984 prole-woman beat as a payoff.*

**Arc**: She does not have one. The change is in Wren, not in her. Her function is *to be noticed*. The dignity she carries is the dignity Wren is learning to see.

## Casting note on absent characters

This is a short story. We don't have a parent on-screen. We don't have a sibling. We don't have a class of forty named friends. The cast is deliberately tight — six people for an hour, which gives each one room to be a full person. Resist the urge to add more. If a story beat needs another body, use the camera-edge approach (the janitor's framing principle): they are *there*, they are real, they don't have to be characters.
